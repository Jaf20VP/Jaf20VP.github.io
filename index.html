Salonica

The impact of technology on religions and society

Great impact of technology

Faith and freedom, body and finitude are some of the topics discussed in the most recent sessions of the second international congress of the magazine *Theologia*, organized by the Orthodox Church of Greece, which concludes today. An open debate between theologians and scholars to outline the challenges of the digital age.

The lens to observe the digital dimension has always been theological, without forgetting, however, to outline the challenges that this dimension imposes on the present. Challenges that other religions must also face, as highlighted by Michalis Marioras, professor at the National and Kapodistrian University of Athens (Nkua), in his intervention on the Muslim faith and the limits of artificial intelligence. Marioras explained the position of Islam, which, when allowing or prohibiting something, uses as a reference the *maslah*, a concept in Sharia law that relates to public interest or community welfare. Chrysostomos (Savvatos), Metropolitan of Messenia and professor at Nkua University, drew attention to the patristic response to the surrounding world and warned of the danger technology represents to human freedom. “Freedom is a substantial concept for man,” he told Vatican media, “because it determines good or bad relations with God and the environment. For my will to be just it must be aligned with God’s freedom, according to the theology of the Fathers.” Technology, on the other hand, comes to “occupy” man, in some way controlling him, influencing his freedom to act and relate to the world.

The arrival of this new technological era represents a change in the anthropological system, reflected in society on different levels. The algorithms that regulate social media platforms or search engines also impact democracy, and this is where governance comes into play. States and political power, however, have no role in this process. This was stated to Vatican media by Evangelos Venizelos, professor of Constitutional Law at Aristotle University of Thessaloniki: “The big problem is that technology transcends state borders, transcends international law, transcends the competences of international organizations, so in reality it shapes a universe of its own, outside political and democratic control.”

At the Salonica congress, professor Lemma gave a lecture on “Body, dependence and finitude in the digital age.”

More tech advances

Helps childhood myopia

Technology to control childhood myopia progression

Myopia, also known as nearsightedness, has become a growing eye health problem, especially among children.

The prevalence of this condition has drastically increased in recent decades, prompting the scientific and medical community to seek effective ways to manage it. The projected prevalence of myopia by 2050 is alarming.

One of the main objections to any new treatment is its effectiveness. However, several studies have shown that Essilor Stellest lenses are effective in controlling the progression of myopia in children.

These lenses have been developed after years of research and solid studies. An article in *Clinical & Experimental Optometry* indicates that the technology behind these lenses is based on proven optical principles and has been adopted by many professionals worldwide, who have endorsed its effectiveness and safety.

Despite objections related to effectiveness, safety, cost, skepticism of innovation, and acceptance, scientific evidence suggests that these lenses are a valid and effective option for managing myopia progression. It is crucial that parents and health professionals evaluate these considerations and become adequately informed about the potential benefits. In a world where myopia continues to rise, technological advances make a difference.

The Muysca Language

Language revival in Colombia

The Muysca language revives in Colombia

For years, the Muysca language was considered extinct, disappearing due to colonial processes and assimilation policies. But today, it is being reborn thanks to the joint efforts of indigenous communities, researchers, and young activists in Bogotá and its surroundings.

In the Indigenous Council of Suba, Muysca classes are offered to children, youth, and adults through community workshops, language games, and participatory methods. A digital platform, Subacubun.com, has also been created to spread the language and facilitate virtual learning.

The recovery of Muysca not only means rebuilding vocabulary or grammar, but also rescuing identity, collective memory, and the worldview of a historically invisibilized people. Furthermore, the process promotes local cultural empowerment, affirming that each language is its own symbolic universe.

This Latin American phenomenon inspires other linguistic revitalization initiatives in the region, showing that “dead” languages can come back to life when communities decide to and institutional support joins in.

Awajún Ceramic Artists Resist

Women defend their ancestral art

Ancestral Art in Peru

In the Peruvian Amazon, specifically in the Cenepa River basin, women of the Awajún ethnic group are leading a cultural struggle against the threat of illegal mining that has contaminated their territories.

These traditional artists have turned ceramic production into an act of resistance and preservation of their collective memory. For decades, they have passed down ancestral techniques from generation to generation. In the last 20 years, they created eight pottery schools to teach these skills to girls and young women.

However, they face difficulties: mercury contamination , scarcity of quality clay, and little institutional support limit their work. In addition, they denounce that illegal mining has generated severe social and environmental impacts in their territories.

This case is a clear example of how culture is not only art or tradition, but also territorial resistance. The Awajún ceramists symbolize the urgency of protecting not only material heritage, but also the lives, ecosystems, and knowledge of indigenous peoples.

Juan Orlando Hernández

A distorted memory?

The Former President of Honduras

The former president of Honduras, Juan Orlando Hernández, sentenced to 45 years in prison for drug trafficking in the United States, has tried to project an image of innocence and resilience from his imprisonment, according to media reports. Recently, he shared on social media an “emotional” message in which he described his experience of running outdoors again in prison, calling it “incredible.” These types of posts, which seek to humanize his image, sharply contrast with the accusations against him and the New York court’s verdict.

On June 26, 2024, a federal court in New York sentenced Hernández to 45 years in prison for his participation in a vast drug trafficking network. The U.S. Department of Justice accused him of using his position as president to facilitate the shipment of tons of cocaine to the U.S., in conspiracy with his brother Tony Hernández and other traffickers. During the trial, prosecutors and witnesses described him as a “two-faced politician” who publicly pretended to fight drug trafficking while secretly conspiring with criminals for political and economic gain.

Hernández’s extradition in April 2022 marked a milestone in Honduras’ history. It was a moment of sharp contrasts: while his supporters cried and claimed his innocence, thousands of Hondurans saw his extradition as a chance to cleanse the “narco-state” that, according to the U.S. verdict, he helped build. Three years later, the conviction solidified the perception that the so-called “war on drugs” he promoted in Honduras was, largely, a farce.

The story of Juan Orlando Hernández reflects the challenges faced by Latin American nations in the fight against corruption and drug trafficking. The phrase “A distorted memory?” encapsulates the complexity of a figure who tried to shape his public narrative but was ultimately unmasked by U.S. justice, revealing a much darker reality than he wanted to project.

General Elections 2025

Everyone must vote

General Elections in Honduras 2025

The general elections in Honduras will be held on November 30, 2025. Citizens will elect a new president, 128 members of the National Congress, and 20 representatives to the Central American Parliament.

Councilor Cossette López expressed her “deep concern” about the actions of security forces inside the CNE facilities. This situation has disrupted the institution’s work during a crucial stage of the electoral calendar.

The complaint came months after the National Congress approved a special budget of more than 85 million lempiras for the Honduran Armed Forces. This budget is intended to cover the costs of guarding, transporting, and monitoring electoral materials — a task the military has traditionally overseen. The participation of the armed forces in elections has long been a topic of political debate.

As the electoral campaign enters its final stage, political tensions are rising. Major parties such as Libre, the National Party, and the Liberal Party have already defined their candidates following the March primaries. Among the presidential hopefuls are Rixi Moncada (Libre) and Salvador Nasralla, who has been an active political figure for years.

Civil society organizations and political analysts have called on authorities and political actors to guarantee transparency in the process. The international community is also paying close attention to Honduras’ political situation given its history of institutional crises. The main goal is to ensure the elections take place peacefully and reflect the will of voters, thereby strengthening Honduran democracy.

Salvador Nasralla

The solution for Honduras

Salvador Nasralla, the Savior of Honduras

As the 2025 general elections approach, Liberal Party presidential candidate Salvador Nasralla intensifies his campaign, focused on transparency, economic development, and defending Honduran sovereignty. With a strong emphasis on economic recovery, Nasralla presents himself as an alternative to traditional parties, highlighting his commitment to progress and real solutions for the people.

In a bid to create more opportunities, Nasralla has placed special emphasis on economic development. During a campaign event in Valle, he outlined key projects including the construction of Amapala Port and the reactivation of the Nacaome dam. These initiatives, according to Nasralla, are crucial for regional growth and job creation.

Another key point in his platform is the defense of national sovereignty, particularly in the Gulf of Fonseca region. He has strongly criticized the current government for failing to protect Honduras’ territorial rights, directly affecting coastal populations. Nasralla has promised that under his administration, defending these rights will be a national priority.

Nasralla has adopted the slogan “Clean Hands” for his campaign, positioning himself as the only candidate free of the corruption that, he claims, has plagued recent governments. This message of honesty and transparency aims to win over disillusioned voters and restore trust in Honduran politics.

With his active presence on social media and tours across the country, Nasralla presents himself as close to the people, listening to their concerns and reaffirming his commitment to a better future for Honduras. Just months before the elections, his message of change and hope continues to gain ground.

Rixi Ramona Moncada

On the road to communism?

Extremist Ideologies?

The campaign of Libre Party candidate Rixi Moncada has been marked not only by her political tour but also by strong criticism from the opposition linking her to extremist ideologies and accusations of dishonesty.

The main criticism surrounding Moncada and her party is the alleged leaning toward communism. Opponents, such as Salvador Nasralla’s party, have labeled her a “communist puppet” and accused her of wanting to “give Honduras away to Venezuela.” Although Moncada promises a “fiscal, economic, and social revolution” if she wins the presidency, critics warn her policies could push the country toward radical socialism, limiting free enterprise and affecting the economy.

Other critics view her as the “chosen heir of the Zelaya-Castro family to inherit the presidential throne.” They argue her candidacy is a maneuver to ensure continuity of the current political project rather than real change in power.

Moncada has also faced accusations of using state media for her political propaganda. In September 2025, the media outlet Contracorriente Honduras reported allegations of “state propaganda in favor of Rixi Moncada,” raising concerns about electoral fairness.

It’s important to note that these criticisms are part of the political debate in Honduras and are rejected by Moncada and her supporters, who instead emphasize her proposals for economic democratization, fighting corruption, and continuing current projects.
